The History of Pringles

Pringles, the beloved potato-based chips, have an interesting origin story that dates back to the 1950s. The invention of Pringles was prompted by consumer complaints about broken, greasy, and stale potato chips. In response to these concerns, Procter & Gamble assigned chemist Fredric J. Baur the task of developing a new kind of potato chip in 1956.

For two years, Baur conducted experiments and eventually devised a solution: saddle-shaped chips made from fried dough. However, despite his breakthrough, Baur faced a challenge in making the chips palatable. As a result, he was taken off the project.

Fortunately, Alexander Liepa joined the team in the mid-1960s to continue Baur’s work. Liepa successfully improved the taste of the chips, earning him credit for the patent. Meanwhile, Baur’s contributions extended beyond flavor enhancement. He also designed the iconic tubular can that is synonymous with Pringles. The unique shape of the Pringles chips is mathematically known as a hyperbolic paraboloid.

The innovative design of the Pringles can proved to be a game-changer in the snacking industry. The cylinder shape allowed for stacking, effectively preventing the chips from breaking and going stale. It also provided a resealable lid, ensuring freshness for prolonged periods.

    Pringles Acquisitions by Kellogg’s

    Kellogg’s, a global food company, has strategically pursued acquisitions to expand its product portfolio and enter new markets. As part of its growth strategy, Kellogg’s has made significant acquisitions in recent years. These acquisitions have allowed Kellogg’s to diversify its offerings and tap into emerging trends in the food industry.

    RXBAR – Tapping into the Healthy Snack Market

    In 2017, Kellogg’s acquired RXBAR, a popular protein bar company, for $600 million. This acquisition enabled Kellogg’s to enter the rapidly growing market for healthy snacks. RXBAR is known for its transparent and minimal ingredient list, offering consumers a nutritious and protein-packed snack option. By acquiring RXBAR, Kellogg’s expanded its presence in the health-focused and on-the-go snack category.

    Parati Group – Entering the Latin American Market

    In 2016, Kellogg’s acquired Parati Group, a leading Brazilian food conglomerate. Parati Group is known for its extensive portfolio of biscuits, powdered beverages, and pasta products. This acquisition provided Kellogg’s with a strategic entry point into the Latin American market. Kellogg’s leveraged Parati Group’s established distribution network and local expertise to expand its presence in Brazil and other Latin American countries, strengthening its position in the region.

    Kashi – Strengthening the Health Food Market Share

    In 2000, Kellogg’s acquired Kashi, an organic breakfast cereal and snack company. Kashi is well-known for its commitment to wholesome, plant-based ingredients and sustainable sourcing practices. The acquisition of Kashi allowed Kellogg’s to further bolster its position in the health food market, catering to consumers who seek nutritious and organic options. Kashi’s portfolio includes a variety of cereals, granola bars, and other plant-based snacks.

    Joint Venture with Tolaram Group – Expanding Presence in Developing Country Markets

    Kellogg’s formed a joint venture with the Tolaram Group, a Singapore-based food conglomerate, to enter and expand its presence in developing country markets. The joint venture focused on manufacturing, selling, and distributing Kellogg’s products in West Africa, including Nigeria and Ghana. This partnership allowed Kellogg’s to tap into the growing consumer market in developing countries and leverage Tolaram Group’s established presence in the region.
